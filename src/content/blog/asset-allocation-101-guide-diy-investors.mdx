## What is Asset Allocation?

If you’re a Canadian investor juggling multiple brokerage accounts, you know the frustration of fragmented portfolio views. But have you ever paused to wonder how your assets are actually allocated? Understanding asset allocation for beginners is crucial for building a robust, diversified investment strategy. In essence, asset allocation is the practice of spreading your investments across different asset classes—like stocks, bonds, and cash—to balance risk and reward based on your specific goals, risk tolerance, and time horizon.

## Major Asset Classes Explained

At the heart of asset allocation lies the concept of diversification. Here’s a quick rundown of the major asset classes:

- **Stocks (Equities):** Owning a piece of a company, stocks are generally considered higher risk but offer the potential for higher returns. For Canadian investors, this could involve investing in Canadian stocks listed on the TSX.
- **Bonds (Fixed Income):** These are loans you give to a government or corporation with the promise of repayment plus interest. Bonds are typically less risky than stocks.
- **Cash and Cash Equivalents:** This includes savings accounts and money market funds. They offer the least risk but also the least return.

## How to Choose Your Allocation

Selecting the right asset allocation involves understanding how to allocate your portfolio in a way that aligns with your investment goals and risk tolerance. Here’s a simple approach:

1. **Define Your Goals:** Are you saving for retirement, a home, or perhaps a child’s education?
2. **Assess Your Risk Tolerance:** Can you stomach market volatility, or do you prefer stability?
3. **Determine Your Time Horizon:** Longer time horizons can afford to take more risk.

### Example: A Balanced Portfolio

A common rule of thumb is the 60/40 stock bond allocation, which suits many Canadian investors looking to balance growth and stability. However, your personal situation may call for adjustments based on your unique circumstances.

## Age-Based Guidelines

Investors often wonder how age influences asset allocation. A younger Canadian investor with decades to retirement might lean heavily towards stocks for growth, while someone closer to retirement age might prioritize bonds to preserve capital.

- **In Your 30s:** Consider an aggressive approach, like an 80/20 stock bond split.
- **In Your 40s and 50s:** A moderate allocation, perhaps 70/30, might be appropriate.
- **Approaching Retirement:** A conservative 50/50 or even 40/60 allocation can help protect your savings.

## Rebalancing Basics

Rebalancing is the process of realigning your portfolio to maintain your desired asset allocation. Over time, some assets will outperform others, skewing your allocation. Rebalancing ensures you stick to your strategy.

- **How Often to Rebalance:** Typically done annually or semi-annually.
- **Rebalancing in Canadian Accounts:** Consider tax implications, especially in non-registered accounts. In tax-advantaged accounts like the TFSA or RRSP, rebalancing can be more tax-efficient.

## Conclusion

Understanding asset allocation for beginners is the first step in building a resilient investment portfolio. By diversifying across asset classes and regularly rebalancing, you can better manage risk and aim for your financial goals.
