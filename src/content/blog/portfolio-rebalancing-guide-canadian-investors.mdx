## What is Rebalancing?

Imagine you're a Canadian investor enjoying the warmth of a summer evening in Toronto, sipping on a double-double, and pondering your investment portfolio. You recall that when you first set up your TFSA and RRSP accounts, you carefully selected an asset allocation that matched your risk tolerance and financial goals. Over time, however, market fluctuations can lead to a drift in this allocation. This is where **portfolio rebalancing** comes into play.

Simply put, rebalancing is the process of realigning the weightings of your portfolio's assets. It involves buying and selling portions of your portfolio to maintain your original desired level of asset allocation. For instance, if stocks have outperformed bonds, your portfolio may become stock-heavy, skewing your risk profile. Rebalancing helps you maintain your desired risk-reward ratio.

## The Rebalancing Bonus Explained

You might wonder, “Why bother with rebalancing if my portfolio is doing well?” Enter the **rebalancing bonus**. This concept refers to the potential for enhanced returns and risk management by systematically buying low and selling high. A study by Vanguard showed that a rebalancing strategy can add about 0.35% to portfolio performance annually. While that might not seem like much, over the decades, it can substantially impact your wealth, especially for Canadian retirement accounts like a TFSA or RRSP.

## Calendar vs Threshold Rebalancing

There are two common rebalancing strategies: **calendar-based** and **threshold-based**. 

- **Calendar Rebalancing**: This method involves rebalancing at regular intervals, such as annually or semi-annually. It’s simple and predictable, making it easier to manage across multiple Canadian brokerage accounts. However, it doesn't account for market conditions.

- **Threshold Rebalancing**: Here, you rebalance your portfolio when asset allocations deviate from your target by a certain percentage, say 5%. This strategy can be more efficient as it responds to market movements but requires more monitoring.

Canadian investors might prefer combining both methods for greater flexibility and control, ensuring that neither market conditions nor arbitrary timelines dictate their rebalancing actions entirely.

## Tax-Efficient Rebalancing Strategies

For Canadian investors, tax efficiency is crucial, especially when dealing with non-registered accounts. Here are some strategies to consider:

- **Use Tax-Advantaged Accounts**: Whenever possible, conduct rebalancing within a TFSA or an RRSP to avoid triggering capital gains taxes.
- **Offset Gains with Losses**: If you need to sell assets in taxable accounts, consider selling underperformers to offset gains.
- **Dividend Reinvestment**: Instead of automatically reinvesting dividends, use them to rebalance your portfolio without incurring additional costs.

Remember, tax laws can be complex, and while we aim to educate, it’s wise to consult with a tax professional regarding your specific situation.
