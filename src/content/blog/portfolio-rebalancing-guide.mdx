## What is Rebalancing?

If you're juggling multiple US brokerage accounts or managing a 401(k) with an eye on the US stock market, you've likely heard about rebalancing. But what does it mean, and why should you care? Simply put, rebalancing is the process of realigning the weightings of a portfolio's assets. This involves periodically buying or selling assets to maintain a desired level of asset allocation or risk.

Imagine you started with a portfolio of 60% stocks and 40% bonds. Over time, as stocks outperform bonds, your portfolio might shift to 70% stocks and 30% bonds. Rebalancing would involve selling some stocks and buying bonds to restore the original 60/40 mix.

### The Rebalancing Bonus Explained

Here's where things get interesting. Studies suggest that rebalancing can lead to a "rebalancing bonus"—an enhanced return by systematically selling high and buying low. For example, a study by Vanguard found that a balanced portfolio could see an annualized return increase of up to 0.43% simply by rebalancing.

This bonus is particularly beneficial for American investors with tax-advantaged accounts like IRAs or Roth IRAs, where capital gains taxes don't immediately apply.

## Calendar vs. Threshold Rebalancing

When it comes to deciding when to rebalance your portfolio, two popular strategies emerge: calendar rebalancing and threshold rebalancing.

### Calendar Rebalancing

This approach involves rebalancing your portfolio on a predetermined schedule, such as annually or semi-annually. It's simple and helps keep you disciplined, but it might not always align with market conditions.

### Threshold Rebalancing

Threshold rebalancing focuses on rebalancing whenever your asset allocations drift from their targets by a specific percentage, say 5%. This method can be more responsive to market changes, but requires more frequent monitoring.

## Tax-efficient Rebalancing Strategies

For US investors, tax efficiency is crucial. Consider these strategies:

- **Tax-advantaged accounts:** Rebalance within your 401(k), IRA, or Roth IRA to avoid immediate tax liabilities.
- **Tax-loss harvesting:** Offset gains by selling underperforming assets at a loss.
- **Using new contributions:** Direct new investments into underweight asset classes rather than selling.
